Mixed-use zoning in mid-sized European cities — what's actually working?

47 replies 2,341 views Last active: 2 hours ago Discussion Evidence-based Europe
PlannerFromGhent Verified Urban Planner 1,204 posts
14 October 2025 · 09:12 #1

I've been following the growing literature on mixed-use zoning outcomes in mid-sized European cities (roughly 100,000–500,000 inhabitants) and wanted to open a discussion here on what the evidence actually shows. There's a lot of advocacy for mixed-use in planning circles, but the empirical picture is more complicated than the standard narrative.

My question: which cities have evidence of measurable outcomes from mixed-use rezoning — not just anecdotal "vibrancy" claims, but things like reduced private car mode share, lower commercial vacancy rates, improved walkability scores, or measurable housing cost moderation?

I'll start by citing the Ghent North Dok study (Van Acker et al., 2023, European Planning Studies) which tracked a 12-year rezoning process. Key findings: commercial vacancy dropped from 31% to 8% in the rezoned corridor; cycling mode share for work trips in the district rose from 24% to 41%; ground-floor activation hours (storefront usage) increased across all seasons. The mechanism was relatively simple: the city relaxed floor-area ratios for residential development in exchange for mandatory ground-floor commercial use, and used public development rights for a new tram corridor to make the commercial use viable.

What other cases do people have data on?

Great topic. The Vienna case is worth examining because it represents a different policy pathway — rather than rezoning existing single-use areas, the city used mixed-use requirements in new development competitions from the 1990s onward. The Seestadt Aspern development (ongoing, eventual capacity ~20,000 residents) has a mandatory 25% non-residential ground floor requirement and a jobs-to-residents ratio target of 1:2.

The 2024 mid-term evaluation (Stadt Wien Stadtentwicklung Wien, 2024) shows mixed results: commercial activation in the completed phases is strong where the tram extension reached (phases 1-3), but ground-floor units in phase 4 (without tram connection yet) have 29% vacancy. This is strong evidence that transit access is not supplementary to mixed-use policy — it is the precondition for commercial viability. Ground-floor retail needs footfall; footfall in mid-sized cities overwhelmingly comes from transit stops, not car parking.

The counter-case that I always raise: Harbertsma district in Groningen, where mixed-use rezoning in 2017 produced almost no commercial uptake despite being in a cycling-heavy city. The issue there was that the zoning change preceded any investment in public space quality — the street design remained car-dominated, and no commercial operator wanted to open facing a four-lane road even with permissive zoning.

The housing cost angle is worth examining separately. There's a subset of the mixed-use literature that argues for it as a partial tool for housing affordability (essentially: allow residential above commercial reduces land-use conflict and therefore increases residential supply). The evidence from French cities is cautious.

In Lyon's Part-Dieu district, the 2018 restructuring plan included significant mixed-use densification and produced around 3,400 new residential units in a previously commercial-heavy zone by 2024. Did it reduce housing costs? No — prices in the district rose 14% against a city average of 9% over the same period. But this is almost certainly because Part-Dieu is one of Lyon's highest-demand locations, and new supply was absorbed by demand growth rather than moderating prices. The counterfactual (what prices would be without the supply addition) is unknowable from observational data.

The broader point: mixed-use zoning is not a demand instrument. It changes where supply can go, not how much demand there is. If city-wide housing demand is growing faster than total supply, mixed-use rezoning redistributes supply geographically but doesn't reduce overall price pressure.

Can we distinguish between what the research calls "fine-grained" mixed use (ground-floor retail/commercial below residential, at the parcel level) and "coarse-grained" mixed use (different uses in close proximity but in separate buildings)? The outcomes literature is quite different for these two types.

Fine-grained mixed use has strong evidence for walkability and reduced car dependence, but weak evidence for commercial viability in low-footfall locations. It also has negative evidence in some contexts: in Utrecht's Lombok district, mandatory ground-floor commercial produced a row of semi-permanent "temporary" uses (pop-up shops, storage, one-person offices) that satisfied the zoning letter but generated none of the vibrancy the policy intended.

Coarse-grained mixed use — essentially compact neighbourhoods with residential, employment, and retail within 600m — has stronger commercial viability evidence and is probably what most successful European cases actually represent. The "15-minute city" concept, regardless of the political noise around it in some countries, is essentially coarse-grained mixed-use planning with an explicit access-time metric.

PlannerFromGhent Verified Urban Planner 1,204 posts
14 October 2025 · 16:33 #5 (OP)

@CyclistAndPlanner — the fine-grained vs coarse-grained distinction is important and underappreciated. The Ghent North Dok case is primarily coarse-grained, actually: the housing and the retail aren't stacked vertically everywhere, they're in adjacent buildings along an activated streetscape. The ground-floor commercial requirement applied only to the tram corridor frontage, not to every residential building.

I'd add a third category: mixed-use in time. In several Scandinavian cases (Malmö and Tampere come to mind), the policy isn't about stacking uses vertically but about ensuring that ground-floor spaces can flex between uses at different times of day and seasons. A café that becomes an evening restaurant, a co-working space that becomes a community hall on weekends. This requires both permissive zoning and real estate structures that allow flexible leasing — which is itself a significant challenge in countries with long commercial lease norms.

The Tampere case study (Mäkinen & Kukkonen, 2024) is worth reading for anyone interested in the governance and property-rights side of this.

From a Central European perspective: the legacy of communist-era single-function zoning (mono-functional housing estates, separated industrial zones, commercial districts with no residential) is still structuring the physical form of many mid-sized cities. Brno is trying to use its new spatial plan (2023) to introduce mixed-use permissions in three formerly industrial corridors, but we are encountering resistance from fire safety regulations that predate mixed-use construction norms. The code assumes either residential or industrial, not both in the same building, and the approval pathway for exceptions is time-consuming and expensive for small developers.

Has anyone successfully navigated building-code reform alongside mixed-use rezoning? I'm particularly interested in how cities in the Netherlands handled this — Dutch building codes seem much more flexible on mixed-use construction.

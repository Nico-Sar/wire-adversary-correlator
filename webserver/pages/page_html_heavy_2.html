Personal Finance in 2025: How to Build a Three-Fund Portfolio from Scratch

The three-fund portfolio has been the cornerstone recommendation of passive investing for thirty years. Here is why it still works, and exactly how to implement it today.

Marco Delgado

Former pension fund analyst. CFA Charterholder. Writing about index investing since 2018.

TL;DR: Open a low-cost brokerage, buy a total-market equity fund, an international equity fund, and a bond fund in proportion to your age and risk tolerance. Rebalance once a year. That is almost everything you need to know.

If you search for investing advice online you will find an overwhelming quantity of noise: options strategies, individual stock picks, leveraged ETFs, crypto thesis documents, and intricate spreadsheets modelling scenarios that will never occur. Almost none of it is useful to someone simply trying to build wealth over a 20 or 30 year horizon.

The three-fund portfolio cuts through all of it. Developed by Taylor Larimore and the Bogleheads community, and endorsed by virtually every respected academic who has studied retail investor outcomes, the strategy is so simple it fits on an index card — and that simplicity is a feature, not a bug.

What the Three Funds Are

The three-fund portfolio consists of:

  1. A total domestic equity index fund — owns essentially every publicly traded company in your home country. In the US, this is something like VTSAX or VTI. In the EU, an equivalent is the Vanguard FTSE All-World UCITS ETF (VWRL) restricted to domestic holdings.
  2. A total international equity index fund — owns developed- and emerging-market equities outside your home country. This provides geographic diversification and reduces single-country risk. For US investors: VXUS or VTIAX. For European investors: VWRL itself is a convenient single-fund substitute for both funds combined.
  3. A total bond market index fund — provides a lower-volatility anchor and rebalancing mechanism. For US investors: BND or VBTLX. For Europeans: Vanguard Global Aggregate Bond UCITS ETF (VAGP) or equivalent.

Why Three Funds and Not One?

Some investors use a single global equity fund (VWRL in Europe, VT in the US) and one bond fund. This is perfectly valid. The reason for separating domestic and international equities is that it gives you explicit control over your home-country allocation, which matters for currency risk, tax treatment, and psychological comfort during domestic market downturns.

Research by Elroy Dimson, Paul Marsh, and Mike Staunton in the Credit Suisse Global Investment Returns Yearbook consistently shows that a globally diversified equity portfolio reduces annualised volatility by 3–5 percentage points relative to a single-country allocation, with no corresponding reduction in long-run expected return. The free lunch is not entirely free — international funds carry currency risk and slightly higher expense ratios — but over a 20-year horizon the diversification benefit consistently dominates.

Choosing the Right Allocation

There is no universally correct allocation. The classic heuristic is to hold your age in bonds: a 30-year-old holds 30% bonds, a 60-year-old holds 60%. This rule of thumb is conservative by modern standards; with longer life expectancies and lower bond yields, many financial planners now suggest subtracting 10 or 20 from your age before applying the rule.

A more principled approach asks: what is the maximum portfolio drawdown you could tolerate without selling? If you could watch your portfolio drop 50% and hold on, you can tolerate a 90% equity allocation. If a 20% drop would cause panic-selling, you need more bonds.

Selecting a Broker

Your broker choice matters primarily because of costs and fund availability. In Europe, the dominant low-cost platforms are DEGIRO, Trade Republic, and Interactive Brokers. In the US, Fidelity, Vanguard, and Schwab offer zero-commission index fund investing. Avoid brokers that charge custody fees exceeding 0.15% per year or that levy fees per dividend reinvestment.

Tax-Advantaged Wrappers

Wherever possible, hold your three funds inside a tax-advantaged account. In the US, the priority order is: employer 401(k) match → Health Savings Account (HSA) → Roth IRA → 401(k) to maximum → taxable brokerage. In Belgium (relevant to many readers), the Pension Savings Fund (PEP) offers a 25% to 30% tax deduction on contributions up to €1,350 per year. The UK ISA allowance is £20,000 per year with zero tax on growth and withdrawals.

Rebalancing

Rebalance once per year, or whenever any asset class drifts more than 5 percentage points from its target. Rebalancing in a tax-advantaged account is simple — just sell high and buy low with no tax consequences. In a taxable account, prefer rebalancing by directing new contributions to underweight asset classes rather than selling, to avoid triggering capital gains.

What About Factor Investing?

Small-cap value tilts, momentum factors, and quality factors all have academic support. They are valid additions to a three-fund core for investors who have the conviction to stick with them through years of underperformance. If you are not certain you have that conviction — and most people are not — the plain three-fund portfolio is the better choice. The enemy of a good plan is the pursuit of a perfect plan.

Getting Started: A Step-by-Step Checklist

  • Open a brokerage account (30 minutes online)
  • Choose your target allocation (equity %, domestic vs international split, bond %)
  • Identify the three cheapest funds available at your broker that cover the three asset classes
  • Set up automatic monthly contributions
  • Set a calendar reminder to rebalance each January
  • Do not check your portfolio more than once per quarter

That is genuinely all there is to it. The hardest part is not the mechanics — it is the behavioural discipline to do nothing when markets crash, and to resist the temptation to tinker when a friend tells you about a stock that doubled last month.

The data is unambiguous: investors who trade frequently underperform those who do not. The three-fund portfolio, held passively over decades, outperforms roughly 90% of actively managed funds after fees. Simple is not unsophisticated. Simple is correct.

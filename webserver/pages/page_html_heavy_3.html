Design and Build

Sony resists the urge to reinvent the visual language of its flagship headphones. The WH-1000XM6 looks almost identical to its predecessor at a glance — matte plastic earcups, a minimalist headband, and the familiar fold-flat mechanism. This is not laziness; it is the sensible recognition that the XM5 design was already excellent in terms of ergonomics, and that most buyers care more about what they hear than what they wear.

The construction is 95% plastic, with only the speaker grilles and headband adjustment sliders in metal. For €379, the all-plastic body raises a mild eyebrow — the Bose QuietComfort Ultra and the Apple AirPods Max both justify their higher prices partly through premium materials. That said, the Sony never feels cheap in the hand; the plastic is well-fitted with no flex or rattle, and it passes the squeeze-and-twist test comfortably.

Clamp force is slightly looser than the XM5, which is a net positive for long listening sessions. The memory-foam ear cushions feel luxurious, and the 40-degree earcup swivel means the headphones adapt to almost any head shape without creating pressure points. After six hours of continuous wear, you notice the weight (248g) but don't hate it.

Active Noise Cancellation

Sony's Dual-layer Adaptive Sound Control v4 is the headline feature, and it remains the best ANC system on a consumer headphone as of this review. Low-frequency rumble — aircraft engines, train carriages, air conditioning units — is attenuated to near-inaudibility. The improvement over the XM5 is most noticeable in the mid-frequency range (200–1000 Hz), where Sony has substantially improved attenuation of conversation-level noise. In an open-plan office, the XM6 renders background chatter genuinely almost silent. The XM5 left a faint but audible trace of it.

The Adaptive mode that automatically transitions between full ANC, Ambient Sound, and off works well in practice. Walking into a coffee shop triggers Ambient mode; stepping back outside restores ANC. It occasionally misreads context — a kitchen ventilator confused it into Ambient mode — but the edge case frequency is low enough not to bother in daily use.

Sound Quality

The default "360 Reality Audio" tuning is warm, slightly bass-boosted, and optimised for streaming. If you use headphones with Spotify or Apple Music at normal listening volumes, you will almost certainly enjoy it out of the box. The low end has authority without bloat; the mids are full and musical; the treble is present but never sibilant.

With LDAC enabled on an Android device (or an iPhone 16 with LDAC support via the latest iOS), resolution improves noticeably. Cymbal textures become crisper; the soundstage widens perceptibly; the microdynamics on well-recorded acoustic instruments are convincingly lifelike. For classical and jazz, LDAC transforms these from "very good consumer headphones" to "headphones that hold their own against many audiophile alternatives twice the price."

Battery and Connectivity

Sony's official 36-hour claim with ANC on is essentially accurate. Testing at 50% volume with ANC on, the XM6 hit 34.5 hours before the low-battery warning. That is about 20% better than the XM5, achieved through a new generation of W1 chipset optimisations and a marginally larger cell.

Multipoint pairing supports three simultaneous devices, up from two on the XM5. Switching between a laptop and a phone is seamless; adding a third device (a tablet, for instance) works without drama. The auto-transfer of audio when a phone call arrives is now handled with sub-second latency — noticeably more responsive than any previous Sony flagship.

Conclusion

Sony has refined rather than reimagined, and that is exactly the right call for a product already at the top of its category. The WH-1000XM6 delivers the best ANC available in consumer headphones, class-leading battery life, and a sound signature that satisfies across streaming, hi-res audio, and phone calls. If you are upgrading from an XM4 or earlier, the jump is substantial. If you own an XM5 and are satisfied, the improvements are incremental. For everyone else looking for the best noise-cancelling headphones at this price point in 2025, this is the answer.

Classic Coq au Vin

Coq au Vin is the definitive French braise — a farmhouse dish elevated by Burgundy wine, pearl onions, lardons, and mushrooms into something deeply satisfying. This version builds maximum depth of flavour through a brief overnight marinade and a proper flour-based liaison, producing a sauce that clings beautifully to wide egg noodles or mashed potato.

Prep30 min
Marinade8–24 h
Cook2 h 30 min
Serves4–6
DifficultyIntermediate
★★★★★ (1,204 made it)

Ingredients

For the marinade

  • 1 whole free-range chicken (1.8–2 kg), jointed into 8 pieces, or 8 bone-in thighs
  • 750 ml Burgundy or other full-bodied red wine (Pinot Noir, Côtes du Rhône)
  • 2 carrots, roughly chopped
  • 1 onion, halved
  • 4 garlic cloves, bruised
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 4 sprigs fresh thyme
  • 1 tsp black peppercorns, cracked

For the braise

  • 3 tbsp plain flour, seasoned with salt and pepper
  • 3 tbsp unsalted butter, divided
  • 2 tbsp neutral oil
  • 200 g lardons (thick-cut pancetta or smoked bacon, cut into batons)
  • 250 g pearl onions or small shallots, peeled
  • 400 g chestnut mushrooms, quartered
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 2 tbsp tomato purée
  • 200 ml chicken stock (homemade or good-quality shop-bought)
  • 1 bouquet garni (thyme, bay, parsley stems)
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper
  • Flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped, to finish

Equipment

  • Large glass or ceramic bowl (for marinating)
  • 28–30 cm enamelled cast-iron casserole (Dutch oven)
  • Large frying pan
  • Slotted spoon
  • Fine-mesh sieve

Method

  1. Marinate the chicken

    Place the chicken pieces in a large glass or ceramic bowl with all the marinade ingredients. Stir to combine, ensuring the chicken is fully submerged. Cover and refrigerate for a minimum of 8 hours, ideally overnight. The wine tenderises the meat and infuses it with the aromatics; longer is better up to 24 hours.

  2. Prepare the garnish

    Remove the chicken from the marinade and pat completely dry with kitchen paper — residual moisture will prevent proper browning. Strain the marinade through a fine-mesh sieve into a jug, reserving both the liquid and the vegetables separately. Discard the thyme sprigs and bay leaves; keep the carrots and onion.

    In a large frying pan over medium-high heat, fry the lardons in 1 tbsp butter until golden and crisp. Remove with a slotted spoon and set aside. In the same fat, colour the pearl onions until lightly caramelised, about 8 minutes. Remove and set aside. Fry the mushrooms in batches (do not crowd the pan) in the remaining fat until they release their moisture and turn golden-brown. Remove and set aside. This trio of garnishes — lardons, onions, mushrooms — should each be cooked separately so they develop their own flavours rather than steaming together.

  3. Brown the chicken

    Dust the chicken pieces lightly in the seasoned flour, shaking off any excess. Heat the remaining butter and oil in the casserole over high heat. Working in two batches, brown the chicken deeply on all sides — 4–5 minutes per side, not moving the pieces until they release naturally. Take your time here; the Maillard reaction is building flavours that cannot be achieved any other way. Transfer the browned chicken to a plate.

  4. Build the braising liquid

    Pour off all but 2 tbsp of the fat from the casserole. Over medium heat, add the reserved marinade vegetables and sauté for 3 minutes. Add the minced garlic and tomato purée and cook for 1 minute, stirring constantly. Pour in the reserved marinade liquid and the chicken stock. Bring to a boil, scraping up all the fond (the browned bits on the bottom of the casserole). Add the bouquet garni.

  5. Braise in the oven

    Return the chicken to the casserole, skin-side up. The liquid should come about halfway up the chicken pieces; add more stock if needed. Bring to a gentle simmer on the stovetop. Cover with the lid and transfer to an oven preheated to 160°C (140°C fan). Braise for 1 hour 15 minutes to 1 hour 30 minutes, until the chicken is completely tender and pulling away from the bone but not falling apart. A cake tester inserted into the thigh joint should meet no resistance.

  6. Finish the sauce

    Remove the casserole from the oven. Transfer the chicken to a warm platter and keep covered with foil. Strain the braising liquid through the fine-mesh sieve into a wide saucepan, pressing on the solids to extract all the juice. Discard the vegetables and bouquet garni. If the sauce seems thin, boil it hard for 5–10 minutes until it coats the back of a spoon and has a glossy, concentrated appearance. Taste and adjust seasoning.

  7. Assemble and serve

    Return the chicken, lardons, pearl onions, and mushrooms to the casserole. Pour the finished sauce over everything and heat gently for 10 minutes to marry the flavours. Scatter generously with flat-leaf parsley and serve directly from the casserole. Wide egg noodles (pappardelle or Alsatian spaetzle), buttered mashed potato, or crusty bread are ideal accompaniments.

Cook's Notes

  • Wine selection: Use a wine you would actually drink. The classic choice is Burgundy (red Burgundy = Pinot Noir), but a good Côtes du Rhône, Beaujolais, or even a Pinot Noir from Alsace works well. Avoid thin, acidic wines — they produce a harsh sauce.
  • Chicken jointing: If you prefer not to joint a whole chicken, bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs are an excellent substitute. They are more forgiving of longer braising times and produce a richer result than breast meat.
  • Make-ahead: Coq au Vin improves significantly made a day in advance. Reheat gently, covered, in a 150°C oven for 30 minutes, or on the stovetop over low heat.
  • Storage: Keeps refrigerated for 3 days, or frozen for 3 months.

Wine Pairing

The obvious choice is to drink what you cook with: a village-level red Burgundy, such as Gevrey-Chambertin or Volnay, brings earthy mushroom and dark fruit notes that echo the dish. For a more everyday option, a Côtes du Rhône from a reliable producer (Château Rayas, Jean-Luc Colombo) offers similar structure at a more accessible price. Avoid heavily oaked New World Pinots — their sweetness clashes with the savoury depth of the braise.

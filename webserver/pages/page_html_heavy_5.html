The Alentejo Coast: Portugal's Best-Kept Secret

While the Algarve fills up and Porto becomes its own cliché, the 130-kilometre Alentejo littoral remains gloriously undervisited — a coast of ancient dunes, whitewashed fishing villages, and Atlantic swell that reaches the shore without having been softened by any other land mass.

Overview

The Costa Alentejana stretches from Setúbal's Arrábida Natural Park in the north to the Sagres promontory in the south, where it meets the Algarve. For administrative purposes, "Alentejo Littoral" refers specifically to the stretch from the Sado estuary near Comporta south to Odeceixe on the River Mira — a region of about 130 kilometres that belongs to the Alentejo district rather than the Algarve.

The topography is distinctive: long, straight Atlantic beaches backed by ancient dune systems, pine forests planted in the 19th century to stabilise the sand, and the mouths of several west-flowing rivers creating wildlife-rich estuaries. The Sado Estuary near Comporta hosts one of Europe's most southerly populations of bottlenose dolphins; the Mira Estuary around Vila Nova de Milfontes is a nesting ground for rare shore birds.

The key distinction from the Algarve is geological: the Alentejo coast faces directly into the Atlantic with none of the sheltering capes and headlands that create the calm coves further south. The swell is larger, the water colder (averaging 18°C in summer, 5–6°C cooler than the southern Algarve), and the wind more persistent. Surfers love it; sun-lounger tourists typically find the Algarve more comfortable. This self-selection keeps the Alentejo coast relatively uncrowded.

Getting There

The nearest international airport is Lisbon Humberto Delgado (LIS), served by direct flights from most European cities. From Lisbon, drive south on the A2 motorway to Alcácer do Sal, then take secondary roads west toward the coast. Journey time to Comporta is about 1h45 without traffic; to Vila Nova de Milfontes, allow 2h30.

There is limited public transport along the coast. A daily bus service connects Lisbon to Vila Nova de Milfontes (about 4 hours), but connections to the smaller villages and beaches require a car or bicycle. Renting a car in Lisbon is strongly recommended for any itinerary covering more than one or two locations. Roads are generally good, though some tracks to remote beaches are unsurfaced.

Alternatively, combine the Alentejo coast with a Lisbon city break: spend three or four days in Lisbon, pick up a rental car, and drive south for a week. The coast and city complement each other well, and the drive through the cork-oak forests of the Alentejo interior — particularly the section between Alcácer do Sal and Santiago do Cacém — is beautiful.

Where to Stay

Comporta

Comporta has become fashionable among wealthy Lisbonites and international visitors over the last decade, but it retains its village character. The accommodation options range from design-conscious boutique hotels (Herdade da Comporta, Torre de Palma Wine Hotel near Monforte) to self-catering villas through local agencies. Book well in advance for July and August; the rest of the year, rooms are easier to find. Prices are higher than elsewhere on the coast.

Vila Nova de Milfontes

The most developed town on the Alentejo coast, with a good range of accommodation from surf hostels to mid-range hotels. The town sits on the north bank of the Mira estuary, offering sheltered river beaches alongside the exposed Atlantic ones. Several excellent seafood restaurants line the riverfront. A good base for exploring the southern Alentejo coast by car.

Zambujeira do Mar

The most remote of the main coastal villages, Zambujeira sits at the top of a dramatic cliff above a beautiful crescent beach. Accommodation is mostly small guesthouses and private rooms. The annual Sudowest Festival (Portugal's biggest independent music festival) takes place here every August, transforming the village for a week.

The Beaches

Praia da Comporta is the most famous, a 30-kilometre strand of fine white sand backed by pine trees and rice paddies. At low tide, the beach is wide enough to feel genuinely deserted even in summer. Facilities are concentrated at the southern end near the village. Flags are posted daily indicating swimming safety — heed them; the currents are powerful.

Praia de Galapinhos, within the Arrábida Natural Park, is often listed among Europe's most beautiful beaches. Access is on foot only (30 minutes from the car park), and visitor numbers are capped by permit in summer. The water here is unusually warm and clear by Atlantic standards, sheltered by the Arrábida limestone cliffs.

Praia de Odeceixe is unique: the beach sits at the mouth of the River Seixe, with a freshwater river swimming area behind the dune line and the full Atlantic in front. Families with children are drawn to the calmer river section. The clifftop village of Odeceixe overlooks the whole scene.

What to Eat

The Alentejo is one of Portugal's great food regions. The coast adds seafood to an already rich inland tradition of pork, game, and legumes. Look for açorda alentejana — a bread-based soup flavoured with coriander, garlic, and a poached egg — alongside the standard grilled fish. Percebes (goose barnacles), harvested from the coastal rocks, are a prized delicacy at a premium price. Cataplana — a copper-pot seafood stew — is present along the whole coast and varies in quality from excellent to tourist-trap depending on the establishment.

The local wine, Vinho do Alentejo, is produced inland from Évora and Reguengos de Monsaraz. Reds based on Alicante Bouschet, Aragonez (Tempranillo), and Trincadeira are full-bodied and approachable; whites from Antão Vaz are refreshing and aromatic. Herdade do Esporão, Herdade Grande, and Quinta do Crasto produce consistently excellent bottles at reasonable prices in local supermarkets.

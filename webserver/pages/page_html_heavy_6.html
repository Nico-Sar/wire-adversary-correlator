Real Madrid once again refused to lose a Champions League final. Jude Bellingham's towering header from a Lucas Vázquez cross in the fourth minute of stoppage time gave Carlo Ancelotti's side a 2–1 victory over Paris Saint-Germain, sending Allianz Arena — split almost evenly between the two sets of supporters — into euphoria and despair in equal measure.

It was, in the end, characteristic of a club whose identity is inseparable from late drama. Ousmane Dembélé had equalised on the hour after a scintillating first-half display by Vinícius Júnior, and for thirty minutes it looked as though PSG's improved second-half structure might hold for a penalty shootout. Then Bellingham arrived at the far post. It always seems to be Bellingham.

First Half: Madrid's Press Dominates

Madrid began the final with a ferocity of pressing that seemed to surprise even Luis Enrique's well-drilled side. The trigger was the PSG goalkeeper — whenever Gianluigi Donnarumma received the ball under pressure, Kylian Mbappé (wearing his new Madrid shirt in the stands, to the delight of the home section) would have watched his former teammates panic-clear to the flanks where Madrid's wide players were already hunting.

The goal came from exactly this pattern. A panicked clearance from Marquinhos fell to Tchouaméni 25 metres out; he drove forward, slipped it left to Vinícius, who had drifted inside from the left flank. The Brazilian feinted once, forcing Hakimi to commit, then hit a low left-footed shot across Donnarumma and inside the far post. It was his twelfth Champions League goal in a single season, a record for a non-striking player.

PSG created very little in the first 45 minutes. Dembélé received the ball in dangerous positions twice but elected for showmanship over shots. Gonçalo Ramos won two aerial duels and lost six. The half-time whistle came with Real Madrid in almost complete control, their xG at 1.8 against PSG's 0.3.

Second Half: Dembélé Changes the Game

Luis Enrique made no substitutions at half-time but shifted his team's shape. Bradley Barcola dropped deeper to press the Madrid right-back aggressively; Vitinha operated higher up the pitch. The effect was immediate. Madrid's press broke down twice in quick succession, and on the second occasion, Barcola drove to the byline and cut back for Dembélé, who arrived unmarked at the penalty spot and side-footed past Courtois.

The goal changed the tactical picture completely. PSG now had a reason to defend; Madrid had to open up. Ancelotti brought on Rodrygo for Camavinga — a forward for a midfielder — and deployed the Brazilian in a more central position to try to recreate the overloads that had been so effective in the first half. It didn't quite work. PSG, with a deeper defensive block, were more compact and Bellingham was being double-marked at every set piece.

Stoppage Time and the Inevitable

With four minutes of stoppage time showing on the board, Lucas Vázquez — on since the 78th minute, principally to shore up the right flank — collected the ball in a deep position and played it the short way to Luka Modrić, who, at 39 years and 241 days, became the oldest player ever to feature in a Champions League final. Modrić had barely touched the ball all night but knew exactly what to do: he rolled it back to Vázquez and made a diagonal run toward the six-yard box. Vázquez's cross was overhit for Modrić but found Bellingham, who had peeled off his marker at the last second. Bellingham's header was precise, powerful, and struck from inside the six-yard box. Donnarumma had no chance.

The bench erupted. Ancelotti, characteristically impassive for most of the night, pumped both fists toward the Madrid supporters. Bellingham sprinted toward the corner flag, removed his shirt, and was booked — he did not care.

What This Means

Real Madrid's 16th European Cup puts them four titles clear of their nearest rivals, Milan and Liverpool on six each. It is Ancelotti's fifth European Cup as a manager — also a record — and Modrić's fifth as a player. For PSG, it is a fifth Champions League final defeat without a victory in the competition; a painful continuation of the club's inability to convert its vast resources into European glory.

The debate about the best Champions League final ever will occupy football media for weeks. What cannot be disputed is that the 94th-minute winner, the tactical narrative of a half-time reset that nearly worked, and the presence of Modrić at 39 made for theatre that the format rarely delivers. Munich 2025 will be replayed on screens for a long time.
